Clinical Rotation in Reproductive Medicine

A six-week clinical-immersion rotation in a working IVF unit, for medical students, new-start doctors and nurses — program skeleton and delivery framework.
Duration 6 weeks · Site Flinders Fertility, Glenelg · Audience students · new-start doctors · nurses · Nominated supervisor A/Prof Tobias Angstmann · Version 2.1

OverviewPurpose and point of difference

What this rotation is, and why it is built the way it is.

This rotation gives medical students structured clinical immersion in reproductive medicine within a working IVF unit — real patients, real follicle tracking, theatre, and the embryology laboratory. It pairs a six-module online learning library with hands-on clinical experience documented in an EPA-based logbook.

The immersion is the deliberate point of difference. Online-only courses can teach the science; they cannot put a student in theatre for oocyte retrieval, at the ultrasound console tracking follicles, or in the room for a difficult prognosis conversation. The logbook exists to document exactly that — and it is the evidence base for building this rotation toward a graduate certificate and, ultimately, a Master of Reproductive Medicine.

The strategic arc

The content library is modular by design: the same six modules and eight EPAs that run this rotation are the reusable building blocks for a graduate certificate and a future masters. Prove delivery and satisfaction here; build the paying-cohort case separately. This document is the top-level map of that library.

CadenceStandard weekly rhythm

Two fixed teaching anchors bracket a week of clinical immersion.
  • Monday AM — module launch (fixed). Tutorial introducing the week's module, its learning objectives, and the EPAs in focus. Students complete the online module through the week.
  • Tuesday–Friday — clinical immersion. Placement across clinics, theatre, laboratory, ultrasound and MDT as the week's focus dictates; students log encounters against the relevant EPAs as they go.
  • Friday PM — review & reflection (fixed). Case review, logbook check and supervisor feedback, module quiz, and a weekly structured reflection (Gibbs) feeding the capstone.

CurriculumThe six modules

Each is a standalone online learning resource with diagrams, worked clinical examples, and a CREI-level reading list.
  1. Reproductive Physiology & Endocrinology — the HPO axis, folliculogenesis, oogenesis and the menstrual cycle; the physiology every later module applies.
  2. Infertility Assessment & Initial Management — defining and investigating infertility in both partners; the diagnostic categories and the escalation of management.
  3. ART Fundamentals — Stimulation, IVF & the Laboratory — stimulation protocols, trigger and OHSS prevention, oocyte retrieval, IVF vs ICSI, embryo culture and grading, fresh vs frozen transfer, and PGT.
  4. Ultrasound in Reproductive Medicine — transvaginal ultrasound, follicle tracking, endometrial and adnexal assessment, common pathology, and early-pregnancy viability.
  5. Complex Cases — Endometriosis, Male Factor & Advanced ART — advanced endometriosis, azoospermia and its genetics, surgical sperm retrieval, recurrent implantation failure, poor response and donor gametes.
  6. Ethics, Law, Communication & Professional Practice — the four principles, the Australian and SA regulatory framework, consent, donor and surrogacy, breaking bad news, and reflective practice (capstone).

◆ On the targets

Targets are deliberately honest for a six-week student rotation. History-taking, interpreting the workup and explaining a plan can reach "supervised"; ultrasound and MDT are assistance-level; oocyte retrieval is observation-level (an understanding EPA). A logbook claiming independence in egg collection after six weeks would not survive accreditation review — restraint here is what makes the record credible to the university and to RTAC.

Sign-offAssessment

Knowledge, documented clinical participation, and integrative reflection.
  • Module quizzes — 15 single-best-answer MCQs per module, drawn from a 90-question bank with teaching rationales.
  • EPA logbook — encounters logged and supervisor-signed across all eight EPAs against their targets.
  • Capstone reflection — ~800 words, structured with Gibbs' reflective cycle, on a case or moment from the rotation.
  • Case presentation — ~10 minutes to the team, integrating clinical reasoning with the professional and ethical dimensions (the EPA 6 showcase).

StandardsGovernance & guidelines

The clinical and regulatory sources the curriculum is built on.

Clinical practice guidance referenced in the modules

Module content is grounded in current international and Australian clinical guidance and landmark trials — chiefly ESHRE, ASRM, NICE and RANZCOG guidelines, the WHO laboratory manual (6th ed, 2021) for semen analysis, and the 2023 international PCOS guideline. Evolving-evidence topics (PGT-A, freeze-all, endometrial receptivity testing, endometriosis surgery) are taught from the primary randomised evidence, and each module carries a CREI-level reading list.

Regulatory & ethical framework

Practice is governed by a layered framework: Commonwealth embryo and cloning law (Prohibition of Human Cloning for Reproduction Act 2002; Research Involving Human Embryos Act 2002; Maeve's Law 2022 for mitochondrial donation); the NHMRC Ethical Guidelines on ART (2017, updated 2023); the RTAC Code of Practice (2024), administered by the Reproductive Technology Accreditation Committee of the Fertility Society of Australia and New Zealand and required for Medicare eligibility; and South Australian law — the Assisted Reproductive Treatment Act 1988 with Regulations 2024 (including the Donor Conception Register and the retrospective donor-identity provisions commencing 26 February 2025) and the Surrogacy Act 2019. These are covered in depth in Module 6.

Why the logbook is the centrepiece

Everything distinctive about this rotation — clinical immersion in a working unit — is what the logbook documents. A student who has logged, with supervisor sign-off, time in theatre, at the ultrasound console, in the MDT and in difficult consultations holds evidence no online cohort can produce. That evidence is what supports the case to the university and to RTAC when advancing the graduate certificate and Master of Reproductive Medicine.
