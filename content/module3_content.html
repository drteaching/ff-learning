The week the immersion pays off

This is the module an online-only competitor structurally cannot deliver. You will attend theatre for oocyte retrieval, stand in the embryology laboratory as oocytes are identified and embryos graded, follow a stimulation cycle across real monitoring visits, and watch an embryo transfer. The reading here exists to make those experiences legible — so that when you see a trigger decision called or a "4AA blastocyst" recorded, you know exactly what is happening and why.

Every intervention in this module is a manipulation of the physiology from Module 1. Stimulation overrides the FSH threshold that normally selects a single follicle. The trigger substitutes for the natural LH surge. Luteal support replaces a corpus luteum the process has disrupted. Keep that lens: ART is applied reproductive physiology, not a separate set of rules.

Documentation matters this week. Your logbook OPU case entry and lab reflection require you to have watched carefully and asked the right questions. Read the relevant section before each clinical session so your observation is informed rather than passive.

1Why stimulate the ovaries at all

The logic follows directly from Module 1's follicle-selection mechanism.

Recall from Module 1 that in a natural cycle, FSH rises, recruits a cohort of antral follicles, then falls — and that falling FSH selects a single dominant follicle (the most FSH-sensitive survives; the rest undergo atresia). IVF needs more than one egg per cycle to be efficient, so controlled ovarian stimulation (COS) deliberately defeats this selection: sustained high-dose exogenous FSH keeps the whole recruited cohort growing rather than letting all but one die. The aim is a controlled number of mature follicles — enough to give a reasonable oocyte yield, not so many that the patient tips into ovarian hyperstimulation.

The second problem stimulation must solve is the premature LH surge. As multiple follicles produce rising oestradiol, that oestradiol can trigger an early endogenous LH surge (the positive-feedback flip from Module 1), causing ovulation before eggs can be collected. So every stimulation protocol also includes a way to suppress the pituitary and prevent that surge — this is what distinguishes the two main protocols.

2Stimulation protocols

Two dominant approaches, differing in how they prevent the premature LH surge. The antagonist protocol is now first-line for safety.

GnRH antagonist protocol (first-line)

Stimulation with FSH begins early in the cycle (day 2–3). A GnRH antagonist is added once follicles reach a certain size (typically around day 5–6 of stimulation); it blocks the pituitary GnRH receptor immediately, preventing the LH surge without a lengthy lead-in. When lead follicles reach ~17–18 mm, final maturation is triggered and oocytes are collected ~36 hours later. The 2020 ESHRE ovarian-stimulation guideline makes a strong recommendation for the antagonist protocol over agonist protocols in the general population, on the basis of comparable efficacy and higher safety — chiefly because it permits a GnRH-agonist trigger and thus dramatically lowers OHSS risk (see section 4).

GnRH agonist "long" protocol

Here a GnRH agonist is started in the mid-luteal phase of the preceding cycle. Recall from Module 1 that continuous (non-pulsatile) GnRH exposure paradoxically desensitises the pituitary — so after an initial "flare," the agonist switches the axis off over about two weeks (down-regulation). Only once suppression is confirmed does FSH stimulation begin, with the agonist continued throughout to hold the pituitary silent. It is effective and still used for specific indications, but the long lead-in and the inability to use an agonist trigger (the pituitary is already suppressed) make it less flexible and higher-risk for OHSS than the antagonist approach.

Other approaches

Mild stimulation uses lower gonadotrophin doses aiming for fewer follicles, and natural or modified-natural cycle IVF uses little or no stimulation — options for particular patients (poor responders, those avoiding OHSS risk, or by preference), trading oocyte yield for lower drug burden.

3Monitoring the stimulated cycle

Ultrasound plus hormones, tracking the cohort toward the trigger. This is EPA 2 in the ART setting.

Once stimulation starts, the patient is monitored every few days with transvaginal ultrasound (counting and measuring follicles, assessing the endometrium) and often serum oestradiol. The two together tell the clinician how the cohort is responding: follicle number and size indicate maturity and the impending oocyte yield, while oestradiol reflects the aggregate granulosa-cell activity (recall the two-cell model — more growing follicles means more aromatase means higher oestradiol).

Monitoring drives three decisions: dose adjustment (increase if under-responding, reduce or consider coasting if over-responding), timing of the trigger (when a satisfactory proportion of follicles reach ~17–18 mm), and safety — an excessive follicle count and steeply rising oestradiol flag OHSS risk and change the trigger and transfer plan. The ESHRE guideline recommends AMH or AFC (from the Module 2 workup) to predict response and set the starting dose before the cycle even begins.

4Triggering final maturation & preventing OHSS

The single most important safety decision in stimulation. Understand it and you understand modern IVF safety.

Follicular stimulation grows the follicles, but the oocytes inside are still immature — arrested, as you know from Module 1, in prophase I. A trigger substitutes for the natural LH surge to drive final maturation (completion of meiosis I) in the ~36 hours before retrieval. There are two trigger options, and the choice is central to safety:

  • hCG trigger: human chorionic gonadotrophin mimics LH but has a long half-life, sustaining stimulation of the ovaries for days after the eggs are collected. Effective, but this prolonged stimulation is what drives ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome in susceptible patients.
  • GnRH-agonist trigger: only possible in an antagonist cycle (the pituitary must be responsive). A bolus of agonist induces a short, physiological LH surge from the patient's own pituitary — enough to mature the oocytes, but short-lived, so the OHSS risk falls dramatically. The trade-off is a defective luteal phase, which is why an agonist trigger is usually paired with a freeze-all strategy (no fresh transfer) or intensive luteal support.

Ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS)

OHSS is the most serious iatrogenic complication of stimulation: an exaggerated response producing enlarged ovaries and a vascular-permeability shift that moves fluid into the third space (ascites, effusions), with haemoconcentration, thromboembolic risk and, in severe cases, organ compromise. Risk is highest in high responders — PCOS, high AMH, many follicles, steeply rising oestradiol. It is largely preventable, and prevention is the whole point of the antagonist-plus-agonist-trigger-plus-freeze-all strategy.

5Oocyte retrieval (OPU)

The procedure you will attend in theatre. Know it well enough to counsel a patient — that is EPA 5.

Oocyte pick-up (OPU), or transvaginal oocyte retrieval, is performed ~36 hours after the trigger, timed so the oocytes have matured but not yet ovulated. Under sedation or light anaesthesia, a needle is passed through the vaginal wall under transvaginal ultrasound guidance into each ovary, and the follicular fluid from each follicle is aspirated. The fluid goes immediately to the adjacent embryology laboratory, where the embryologist identifies the oocytes under the microscope — not every follicle yields an egg, and the retrieved number is usually a little below the follicle count.

For your counselling competence, know the shape of it: a day procedure, ~20–30 minutes, done under sedation; risks include bleeding, infection, and injury to adjacent structures (all uncommon), plus the anaesthetic risk and the background OHSS risk in high responders. Patients are typically home within hours. When you attend, document in your logbook: indication, protocol used, number of follicles aspirated, number of oocytes retrieved, sedation type, and any complications.

◆ Clinical relevance — the follicle-to-oocyte gap

Patients often expect one egg per follicle and are distressed when, say, 12 follicles yield 8 eggs. This is normal — not every follicle contains a mature, retrievable oocyte, and empty-follicle situations occur. Knowing this lets you set expectations before OPU rather than explaining a "shortfall" afterwards. Attrition continues at every subsequent step (fertilisation, cleavage, blastocyst), which is why counselling frames the funnel early.

6The laboratory — IVF versus ICSI

Where fertilisation happens. The IVF-versus-ICSI decision comes straight from Module 1's fertilisation sequence.

In the laboratory the retrieved oocytes meet the prepared sperm by one of two methods. In conventional IVF, prepared sperm are placed in the dish with each oocyte and the natural sequence — capacitation, zona binding, acrosome reaction, penetration (all from Module 1) — is allowed to occur. In ICSI (intracytoplasmic sperm injection), a single sperm is injected directly into the oocyte cytoplasm, bypassing every one of those steps. ICSI is therefore the answer to significant male factor (from Module 2's semen analysis), prior fertilisation failure, or use of surgically retrieved sperm; it is not superior for non-male-factor cases and is not a default.

Fertilisation is checked the next morning (day 1): normal fertilisation shows two pronuclei (2PN) — one maternal, one paternal — as described in Module 1. Abnormal fertilisation (1PN, 3PN) is identified and those embryos are not used. This 2PN check is the first quality gate in the culture sequence.

◆ Clinical relevance — why the lab obsesses over witnessing

Every time gametes or embryos change vessel or ownership, the step is double-witnessed (by a second embryologist or an electronic witnessing system). The reason is the single worst error an IVF unit can make — mixing up the gametes or embryos of two patients. This is why the laboratory feels rule-bound and repetitive; that culture of verification is a patient-safety feature, and it is exactly the sort of quality-assurance principle your lab reflection should engage with. RTAC accreditation (Module 6) exists to enforce it.

7Embryo culture and grading

How the lab decides which embryo to transfer. The Gardner system you will hear quoted, decoded.

Fertilised embryos are cultured, and modern practice usually grows them to the blastocyst stage (day 5–6) before transfer or freezing rather than transferring at cleavage stage (day 3). The rationale, from Module 1: extended culture lets the strongest embryos self-select — many chromosomally abnormal embryos arrest before blastocyst — improving the chance that a transferred embryo is viable.

Blastocysts are scored with the Gardner grading system, which has three components: a number (1–6) for the degree of expansion of the blastocoel cavity and hatching status, then two letters (A–C) grading the inner cell mass (ICM, which becomes the fetus) and the trophectoderm (TE, which becomes the placenta). So "4AA" is an expanded blastocyst with a high-quality inner cell mass and trophectoderm — a good-looking embryo. Grading predicts implantation potential but is a morphological judgement, not a genetic one: a good-grade embryo can still be aneuploid, and a modest-grade embryo can be euploid.

8Fresh versus frozen transfer & luteal support

An area where the evidence has genuinely shifted. Know what the trials actually show.

A blastocyst can be transferred in the same (fresh) cycle or vitrified and transferred later in a frozen embryo transfer (FET). Vitrification — ultra-rapid freezing — has made frozen transfer highly effective, and survival on warming now exceeds 95% in good laboratories. This has driven a large shift toward freeze-all strategies, but the evidence for routine freeze-all is more nuanced than the enthusiasm sometimes suggests.

The landmark randomised trials sort it out. In PCOS/high responders, freeze-all improves live birth and reduces OHSS (Chen et al., NEJM 2016). In ovulatory women without PCOS, freeze-all does not improve live birth (Shi et al., NEJM 2018) — though a later blastocyst-transfer trial did favour FET (Wei et al., Lancet 2019). Across the board, freeze-all reliably reduces OHSS. The reasonable synthesis: freeze-all is clearly indicated for OHSS risk and high responders; for normal ovulatory responders it is a reasonable option but not a universal live-birth upgrade.

Luteal support

Recall from Module 1 that implantation needs progesterone. Stimulation and the trigger disrupt normal corpus-luteum function, so fresh-transfer cycles receive luteal progesterone support. In a medicated FET cycle the endometrium is built with oestrogen and then progesterone is started and counted precisely, transferring a day-5 blastocyst after the equivalent progesterone exposure — reproducing the natural implantation window (Module 1, Figure 6) deliberately.

9Genetic testing of embryos (PGT)

An "add-on" you must be able to discuss critically, because patients will ask and the evidence is contested.

PGT-M (for monogenic disorders) tests embryos for a specific known heritable condition when a couple carries it — an established, high-value indication. PGT-A (for aneuploidy) screens embryos for whole-chromosome abnormalities to select which to transfer, and this is where the controversy lies.

The intuition is appealing — transfer only euploid embryos — but the best trials do not show the expected benefit in general use. In good-prognosis patients, conventional IVF was non-inferior to PGT-A for cumulative live birth (Yan et al., NEJM 2021: 81.8% vs 77.2%), and the STAR trial (Munné et al., 2019) found no overall improvement. PGT-A does reduce miscarriage per transfer and can shorten time to a successful transfer, and post-hoc data suggest benefit in older women (35–40) — but a cumulative-live-birth advantage across all comers is not established, and embryos discarded as "abnormal" (including mosaics that can produce healthy babies) are a real cost. The ASRM's 2024 committee opinion characterises universal PGT-A as unproven for improving live birth across all patients.

◆ Clinical relevance — how to talk about add-ons

PGT-A is the archetypal IVF "add-on": intuitively attractive, widely marketed, expensive, and of contested benefit. The clinical skill is presenting it honestly — what it can do (reduce miscarriage per transfer, possibly help older patients, shorten the path), what it cannot (reliably raise cumulative live birth in good-prognosis patients), and its costs (expense, discarded potentially-viable embryos). Watching how the specialists here counsel on add-ons without overselling is a lesson in evidence-based, non-commercial practice — and directly relevant to the ethics content in Module 6.

◆ Worked interpretation — work through it before the answer
29-year-old with PCOS, undergoing her first IVF cycle on a GnRH antagonist protocol. AMH was 58 pmol/L (high). On the day of planned trigger: 24 follicles ≥12 mm across both ovaries, oestradiol markedly elevated, endometrium 10 mm triple-line. She feels well.

What is the risk, and what trigger and transfer strategy would you expect the team to choose — and why?

Interpretation. This is a classic high-responder, high-OHSS-risk picture: PCOS, high AMH, a large follicle cohort (24), and steeply elevated oestradiol. An hCG trigger followed by a fresh transfer would carry a serious risk of significant OHSS, made worse if she conceives (endogenous hCG sustains the syndrome).

Strategy. Because she is on an antagonist protocol, the safe route is available: a GnRH-agonist trigger (short LH surge from her own pituitary, matures the oocytes, avoids the prolonged hCG stimulus) combined with a freeze-all — vitrify all blastocysts and defer to a frozen transfer in a later, unstimulated cycle once the OHSS risk has passed.

Why it ties together. This is the whole logic of Figure 2 in one patient: the antagonist protocol was the enabling choice, the agonist trigger is the safety lever, and freeze-all removes the pregnancy-driven OHSS risk. And recall Chen et al. — in PCOS, freeze-all also improves live birth, so here it is doubly justified.

✓ Module 3 — the concepts you must carry forward
  • Stimulation defeats natural follicle selection (sustained high FSH) and must prevent a premature LH surge — the job of the antagonist or agonist.
  • The antagonist protocol is first-line for safety: it enables a GnRH-agonist trigger, which — with freeze-all — largely prevents OHSS.
  • The trigger substitutes for the LH surge; hCG is effective but OHSS-prone, agonist trigger is short and safe (antagonist cycles only).
  • ICSI is for male factor and fertilisation failure, not a default; conventional IVF otherwise. 2PN on day 1 confirms normal fertilisation.
  • Blastocyst culture self-selects; Gardner grading (number + ICM letter + TE letter) is morphological, not genetic.
  • Freeze-all clearly helps high responders (OHSS ↓, live birth ↑ in PCOS) but is not a universal upgrade for normal responders.
  • PGT-A does not reliably raise cumulative live birth in good-prognosis patients — counsel add-ons honestly.
