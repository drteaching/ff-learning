Why this module comes first

Everything you will see in the clinic over the next six weeks is an intervention in a physiological system. A stimulation protocol manipulates the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis. An AMH result estimates a follicle population you cannot see. A failed implantation is a breakdown in a precisely timed molecular conversation between embryo and endometrium. If you understand the normal system, the clinical decisions become legible. If you don't, they look like a series of arbitrary rules.

This module is deliberately dense. It is the shared vocabulary for the whole rotation, and the concepts here — the two-cell model, the FSH window, ovarian reserve, the implantation window — will reappear in every subsequent module in progressively more clinical form. Read it once now; you will return to it.

By the end of this module you should be able to explain, to a patient or a colleague, how a normal menstrual cycle is controlled, why ovarian reserve declines with age, what a hormone panel is actually measuring, and why the interventions you will observe in the clinic are designed the way they are. The clinical activities in Week 1 are observational — the goal is to attach what you read here to what you see there.

1Functional reproductive anatomy

A brief review — structure only insofar as it drives function.

You have covered gross anatomy elsewhere. What matters for reproductive medicine is the functional organisation of a few key structures.

The ovary

The ovary has two jobs: it is both an endocrine organ and the store of the female gamete supply. Its cortex contains the entire follicle population — a fixed, non-renewable pool established before birth. Each follicle is an oocyte surrounded by supporting somatic cells (granulosa and, later, theca cells) that do the hormone production. The medulla carries the vasculature and nerves. The clinically important point: the follicle is the functional unit, and the number of follicles remaining is finite and declining from birth onward. This is the substrate of ovarian reserve, the single most important concept in fertility prognosis.

The uterus and endometrium

The endometrium has two layers. The functionalis is the upper layer that proliferates, secretes, and is shed each cycle; it is the layer that must be receptive for implantation. The basalis is the deep regenerative layer that survives menstruation and regrows the functionalis. The myometrium is the muscular wall. When you assess an endometrium on ultrasound in the clinic — thickness, the "triple-line" pattern — you are assessing whether the functionalis has responded appropriately to oestrogen (proliferation) and progesterone (secretory transformation).

The fallopian tubes

Not passive conduits. The tube is the site of fertilisation and of the embryo's first three to four days of development. Ciliary beat and smooth muscle peristalsis transport the oocyte inward and the embryo toward the uterus, timed so the embryo arrives as a morula/early blastocyst when the endometrium is becoming receptive. Tubal damage — the reason tubal patency is assessed in the workup — disrupts both gamete transport and this developmental environment, and a damaged, fluid-filled tube (hydrosalpinx) is actively hostile to implantation.

The male tract

The testis performs the male equivalent of the ovary's dual role. The seminiferous tubules house spermatogenesis, supported by Sertoli cells; the interstitial Leydig cells produce testosterone. Sperm mature and gain motility during transit through the epididymis, are stored, and are delivered via the vas deferens. Accessory glands (seminal vesicles, prostate) contribute the bulk of seminal fluid. This architecture explains the diagnostic logic in male-factor infertility: a blockage (obstructive) produces a very different picture from a production failure (non-obstructive), and you will trace that distinction in Module 5.

2The hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis

The control system. Master this and the menstrual cycle, PCOS, and every stimulation protocol become coherent.

Reproduction is governed by a three-tier hormonal cascade with feedback loops. Get the architecture first, then the cycle.

The cascade

The hypothalamus releases gonadotrophin-releasing hormone (GnRH) in pulses into the pituitary portal circulation. The pulsatility is not incidental — it is the signal. The anterior pituitary gonadotrophs respond to pulsatile GnRH by secreting the two gonadotrophins: FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) and LH (luteinising hormone). These act on the ovary, which in turn produces oestradiol, progesterone, and the peptide hormones inhibin and AMH. The ovarian hormones feed back on both the hypothalamus and pituitary to regulate the whole system.

◆ Clinical relevance — why pulse frequency matters

GnRH pulse frequency encodes different instructions. Slow pulses favour FSH secretion; fast pulses favour LH. This is not academic: continuous (non-pulsatile) GnRH exposure paradoxically shuts down the axis by desensitising the receptors. That is precisely the mechanism exploited by GnRH agonists in IVF down-regulation protocols — you flood the system continuously to switch it off, then control it exogenously. You will see this in Module 3. Altered pulse frequency also underlies the raised LH:FSH ratio characteristic of PCOS.

The menstrual cycle, phase by phase

With the cascade in place, the cycle is a story about a single dominant follicle growing, ovulating, and becoming a corpus luteum. Conventionally counted from the first day of menses (day 1); an "average" cycle is 28 days but 24–35 is normal.

Follicular phase (day 1 to ovulation)

At the start, oestradiol, progesterone and inhibin are low, which releases the pituitary from negative feedback and allows FSH to rise. This FSH rise recruits a cohort of antral follicles into the final growth phase. As follicles grow, their granulosa cells produce increasing oestradiol and inhibin B. Inhibin B selectively suppresses FSH, so FSH falls again mid-follicular phase — and this falling FSH is the mechanism of dominant follicle selection: only the follicle with the most FSH receptors (the most FSH-sensitive) can continue growing as FSH declines. The others undergo atresia. Rising oestradiol from the dominant follicle drives endometrial proliferation.

Ovulation

Here the feedback logic flips. Once oestradiol from the mature follicle exceeds a threshold (roughly >735 pmol/L / ~200 pg/mL) sustained for ~48 hours, it switches from negative to positive feedback, triggering the LH surge. The LH surge does several things over ~36 hours: it completes the oocyte's first meiotic division, triggers final maturation, and causes follicular rupture — ovulation — typically ~36–38 hours after the surge onset. This 36-hour interval is why ovulation timing works clinically, and why the "trigger" injection in IVF is timed the way it is.

Luteal phase (ovulation to menses)

The ruptured follicle luteinises into the corpus luteum, which produces large amounts of progesterone (plus oestradiol and inhibin A). Progesterone transforms the proliferative endometrium into a secretory, receptive state and maintains it. The luteal phase is remarkably constant at ~14 days (cycle length varies through the follicular phase, not the luteal). If no pregnancy occurs, the corpus luteum regresses after ~14 days, progesterone and oestradiol fall, the endometrium is shed (menses), FSH is released from suppression, and the next cycle begins. If pregnancy occurs, embryonic hCG rescues the corpus luteum, sustaining progesterone until the placenta takes over.

✓ Anchor concepts
  • Falling FSH selects the dominant follicle — the survivor is the most FSH-sensitive. In IVF, sustained high exogenous FSH overrides this selection to grow multiple follicles.
  • Oestradiol switches from negative to positive feedback at a sustained threshold — this triggers the LH surge and is the pivot of the whole cycle.
  • The luteal phase is fixed (~14 days); the follicular phase is variable — cycle-length variation lives in the follicular phase.
  • Progesterone makes the endometrium receptive — this underpins luteal support and frozen transfer timing.

3Folliculogenesis and ovarian reserve

The follicle's life story, and the finite pool that defines a patient's prognosis.

A follicle takes far longer to develop than one menstrual cycle. The full journey from a dormant primordial follicle to a preovulatory follicle takes several months; only the last ~two weeks — the antral phase — is gonadotrophin-dependent and coincides with the cycle you can influence.

Ovarian reserve

The primordial pool is established in fetal life — peaking at ~6–7 million around mid-gestation, ~1–2 million at birth, ~300,000–400,000 at puberty — and only ever declines. Fewer than 500 will ever ovulate; the rest are lost to continuous atresia. This decline accelerates after ~age 37. Crucially, it is a decline in both quantity (how many follicles remain) and quality (the oocytes' propensity to be chromosomally normal). Both matter, and they are not the same axis — which is why a young woman with low reserve often has a better prognosis than her egg number alone suggests, and an older woman with adequate reserve still faces the quality problem of age.

◆ Clinical relevance — AMH as a reserve marker

Anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH) is produced by the granulosa cells of pre-antral and small antral follicles — precisely the population that reflects the size of the remaining reserve pool. This makes it the best single blood marker of ovarian reserve, and unlike FSH it is relatively cycle-independent, so it can be measured on any day. In the clinic AMH is used mainly to predict response to stimulation and set the starting FSH dose — high AMH warns of over-response and OHSS risk; low AMH predicts a poor yield.

What AMH does not do well: predict natural fertility or the chance of conceiving in any given month. A patient with low AMH is not infertile; she has fewer eggs, not necessarily worse ones. Communicating this distinction sensitively is a real clinical skill you will see practised — and it is EPA 7.

4Oogenesis and the meiotic arrests

Why maternal age is the dominant variable in fertility — the answer is in the timing of meiosis.

Female gametogenesis is defined by two long pauses. Oogonia proliferate in the fetal ovary and enter meiosis, but then arrest in prophase of meiosis I (the dictyate stage) before birth. Every oocyte a woman ovulates in her lifetime has been sitting in this arrested state since before she was born. That is the central fact.

Meiosis I is only completed at ovulation, triggered by the LH surge, producing a secondary oocyte and the first polar body. The oocyte then immediately arrests again in metaphase of meiosis II, and meiosis II is completed only if fertilisation occurs.

◆ Clinical relevance — the aneuploidy problem

The decades-long prophase I arrest is the mechanistic reason for age-related aneuploidy. The molecular machinery holding the chromosomes together (cohesins) degrades over time. The longer an oocyte has been arrested, the more likely the chromosomes are to segregate incorrectly at the belated completion of meiosis I — producing eggs with the wrong chromosome number. This is why the miscarriage rate and the trisomy rate rise steeply with maternal age, why egg quality (not just quantity) drives IVF success, and why donor eggs from young women largely erase the age effect. When you hear a specialist counsel a 42-year-old about success rates, this is the biology underneath the numbers.

5Spermatogenesis and male reproductive physiology

A continuous production line — the opposite design to the female system.

Where oogenesis is a fixed pool laid down before birth, spermatogenesis is continuous production from puberty onward. One full cycle takes approximately 74 days — a number worth remembering, because it dictates how long any intervention (lifestyle change, treatment of a varicocele, cessation of a gonadotoxin) takes to show up in a semen analysis. Advising a patient to stop smoking and re-test in three months is this number in practice.

The sequence: spermatogonia (stem cells) → primary spermatocytes → (meiosis) → secondary spermatocytes → spermatids → spermatozoa. This occurs within the seminiferous tubules, orchestrated by Sertoli cells, which respond to FSH, nurse the developing gametes, and form the blood-testis barrier that immunologically isolates sperm. Testosterone — produced by the Leydig cells under LH — is required locally at high concentration for spermatogenesis to proceed.

6Fertilisation

The molecular handshake — and where ICSI bypasses it.

Fertilisation is a sequence, not an event. Ejaculated sperm are not immediately capable of fertilising; they must first undergo capacitation in the female tract — a maturation of the membrane and motility (or, in the lab, an equivalent preparation step). A capacitated sperm reaching the oocyte binds to the zona pellucida, the glycoprotein shell. Binding triggers the acrosome reaction: the sperm releases enzymes that digest a path through the zona. The sperm membrane then fuses with the oocyte membrane, and the sperm nucleus enters.

Fusion triggers the cortical reaction — the oocyte releases granules that harden the zona and block any further sperm (the polyspermy block, since fertilisation by more than one sperm is lethal). It also completes meiosis II. The two haploid pronuclei — maternal and paternal — then migrate together and their membranes break down, restoring the diploid chromosome number. The fertilised egg is now a zygote.

◆ Clinical relevance — conventional IVF vs ICSI

In conventional IVF, prepared sperm are placed with the oocyte and this natural sequence — binding, acrosome reaction, penetration — is allowed to happen in the dish. In ICSI (intracytoplasmic sperm injection), a single sperm is injected directly into the oocyte cytoplasm, bypassing capacitation, zona binding and the acrosome reaction entirely. That is why ICSI is the answer to severe male factor: it makes fertilisation possible even when the sperm couldn't accomplish these steps themselves. You will watch both in the lab in Module 3, and understanding this section is what makes that visit meaningful rather than mysterious.

7Early embryogenesis

Days 1 to 6 — the exact window the embryology lab observes and grades.

The zygote undergoes cleavage — successive cell divisions without overall growth, so the cells (blastomeres) get smaller. The rough timeline the lab watches:

At the blastocyst stage two distinct populations emerge: the inner cell mass (which becomes the fetus) and the trophectoderm (which becomes the placenta). The blastocyst then hatches from the zona pellucida, a prerequisite for implantation.

◆ Clinical relevance — why blastocyst transfer and why day 5

Modern practice favours culturing embryos to the blastocyst stage (day 5) before transfer or freezing, rather than transferring at cleavage stage (day 3). Extended culture lets the strongest embryos self-select — many chromosomally abnormal embryos arrest before reaching blastocyst — improving the odds that a transferred embryo is viable. The two cell lineages also matter for genetic testing: PGT-A biopsies a few trophectoderm cells (the future placenta, not the future baby) at blastocyst stage. The Gardner grading system you will hear quoted in the lab scores blastocyst expansion, inner cell mass, and trophectoderm quality. Module 3 covers this in full; you now have the developmental scaffold for it.

8Implantation and the window of receptivity

A timed, two-sided event — the failure point in much of IVF.

Implantation requires a competent embryo and a receptive endometrium, synchronised. The endometrium is receptive only during a narrow window of implantation — roughly days 20–24 of an idealised cycle, about 6–10 days after ovulation (or after the progesterone rise). Outside this window the endometrium will not accept an embryo, however good.

Receptivity is created by progesterone acting on an oestrogen-primed endometrium, driving secretory transformation and decidualisation — the stromal changes that prepare the tissue to support an implanting embryo. Implantation itself proceeds through apposition (the blastocyst settles against the endometrium), adhesion (it attaches), and invasion (the trophoblast penetrates and establishes the placental interface, secreting hCG that rescues the corpus luteum).

◆ Clinical relevance — luteal support and frozen transfer timing

Two clinical practices fall straight out of this. First, luteal phase support: IVF cycles often need supplemental progesterone because the stimulation and egg-collection process can impair natural corpus luteum function — and without adequate progesterone the window never opens. Second, frozen embryo transfer (FET) timing: in a medicated FET cycle the clinic builds the endometrium with oestrogen, then starts progesterone and counts precisely, transferring a day-5 blastocyst after the equivalent of five days of progesterone exposure — synchronising embryo stage to the receptivity window. The whole edifice of transfer timing is this window. When a good embryo fails to implant repeatedly, endometrial receptivity is one of the things reconsidered (Module 5, recurrent implantation failure).

9Hormonal evaluation in practice

The single most clinically applicable section. This is what you will interpret in EPA 2.

A fertility hormone panel is not a random battery — each test answers a specific question. Australian laboratories report in SI units; the reference points below use SI.

The core tests and what they mean

Day 2–3 FSH, LH and oestradiol (basal panel)

Measured early in the follicular phase, when the system is at baseline. A high basal FSH (broadly >10 IU/L, lab-dependent) suggests diminished ovarian reserve — the pituitary is working harder to recruit follicles from a depleted pool. But there is a trap: FSH is only interpretable if oestradiol is also low (roughly <250 pmol/L). A prematurely rising oestradiol from an early-developing follicle suppresses FSH and can mask a poor reserve with a falsely reassuring FSH. Always read the two together. LH is assessed partly for the LH:FSH ratio, classically elevated in PCOS.

AMH (anti-Müllerian hormone)

As covered in section 3: reflects the antral follicle pool, cycle-independent, the workhorse marker for planning stimulation. Australian labs report in pmol/L (note international literature often uses ng/mL; 1 ng/mL ≈ 7.14 pmol/L, so a common cutoff of ~1 ng/mL corresponds to ~7 pmol/L). Interpret AMH as a guide to response and dose, not as a verdict on fertility.

Mid-luteal progesterone

Measured about 7 days after presumed ovulation (day 21 of a 28-day cycle). A level above ~30 nmol/L confirms that ovulation occurred. This is the simplest confirmation of an ovulatory cycle and directs the workup: an anovulatory pattern points toward PCOS, thyroid or prolactin causes.

Prolactin and TSH

Screening tests because both hyperprolactinaemia and thyroid dysfunction disrupt ovulation and are readily treatable. Always checked in an anovulatory or irregular-cycle presentation.

◆ Worked interpretation — try before reading the answer
32-year-old, 14 months of trying, cycles every 45–60 days. AMH 48 pmol/L (high). Day 3 FSH 5 IU/L, LH 12 IU/L, oestradiol 140 pmol/L. Mid-luteal progesterone 8 nmol/L. TSH and prolactin normal.

Work through it yourself first. What does each value tell you? What is the likely diagnosis? What is the reserve like, and what does that mean for treatment risk?

Interpretation. The long, irregular cycles plus a raised LH:FSH ratio (12:5) and a high AMH point to PCOS with anovulation. The low mid-luteal progesterone (8, well under 30) confirms she is not ovulating — consistent with the picture. Reserve is not the problem; it is abundant, which is exactly why the high AMH is a warning flag for OHSS if she is stimulated. Basal FSH and oestradiol are reassuring for reserve.

Direction. This is an ovulation problem, not a reserve problem. First-line management is ovulation induction (letrozole), with lifestyle input, rather than jumping to IVF — and if IVF is ever needed, cautious stimulation because of the OHSS risk her high AMH signals. You will see this logic play out in Module 2.

10Endocrine pathology primer

An orientation only — each condition is developed clinically in later modules.

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)

The most common endocrine cause of anovulatory infertility. Diagnosed by the Rotterdam criteria — two of three: oligo/anovulation, clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism, and polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound — after excluding mimics. The underlying physiology ties back to this module: altered GnRH pulsatility raises the LH:FSH ratio, LH drives theca androgen production (the two-cell model), and insulin resistance amplifies the androgen excess and disrupts follicle maturation, so follicles are recruited but stall before a dominant one is selected — hence the polycystic appearance and anovulation. High AMH is characteristic (large antral pool). Full clinical management is in Module 2.

Thyroid dysfunction

Both hypo- and hyperthyroidism disrupt the menstrual cycle and impair fertility, and thyroid autoimmunity is associated with miscarriage. Hypothyroidism is the more common issue in this setting; it can raise prolactin and disturb ovulation. Because it is easily screened (TSH) and easily treated, it is checked in essentially every workup, and optimising thyroid status before conception is standard.

Hyperprolactinaemia

Elevated prolactin suppresses GnRH pulsatility, causing anovulation, often with oligo/amenorrhoea and sometimes galactorrhoea. Causes range from physiological (stress, medications, hypothyroidism) to a pituitary prolactinoma. It is screened for the same reason as thyroid disease — common, detectable, treatable (dopamine agonists), and reversible as a cause of infertility.

✓ Module 1 — the concepts you must carry forward
  • The HPO axis with pulsatile GnRH and the two-cell model explains the normal cycle and every stimulation protocol you will see.
  • Falling FSH selects one dominant follicle; sustained high oestradiol flips feedback to trigger the LH surge; the luteal phase is fixed at ~14 days.
  • Ovarian reserve is finite and declines in both quantity and quality; AMH estimates the pool and guides stimulation but does not define fertility.
  • The meiotic arrests of oogenesis are the mechanistic basis of age-related aneuploidy — the dominant variable in prognosis.
  • Implantation needs a receptive endometrium (progesterone-driven) synchronised to a competent embryo — the basis of luteal support and transfer timing.
  • A hormone panel is a set of targeted questions: reserve (FSH+E2, AMH), ovulation (mid-luteal progesterone), treatable causes (TSH, prolactin), PCOS (LH:FSH, AMH).
