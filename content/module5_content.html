Where protocols end

Modules 1–4 gave you the standard patient and the standard cycle. This module is about the couples for whom the algorithm runs out — advanced endometriosis, azoospermia, repeated failure, diminished reserve, the need for donor gametes. These cases need reasoning, not recipes: weighing competing priorities, tolerating uncertainty, and coordinating across disciplines. They are also where the biggest counselling and ethical demands live, which is why this week targets the multidisciplinary and psychosocial EPAs.

A theme runs through the whole module: the discipline of not over-treating. Complex, distressed patients are the most vulnerable to expensive, weakly-evidenced "add-ons" — and the mark of a good clinician here is knowing which interventions the evidence actually supports. You saw this with PGT-A in Module 3; you will see it again with surgery-before-IVF and endometrial receptivity testing.

This week, prepare a one-page summary of an MDT case before the meeting and present it if the environment allows (EPA 6). Watch closely how the team holds difficult conversations — failed cycles, poor prognosis, donor recommendations — because recognising and responding to distress is EPA 8, and it is on display in nearly every complex consultation.

1Endometriosis: why it impairs fertility

Not one mechanism but four. Understanding them explains why treatment is so case-dependent.

Endometriosis is the presence of endometrial-like tissue outside the uterus, driving chronic inflammation and, often, subfertility. Its origin is still debated — the classic retrograde menstruation (Sampson) theory is now seen as incomplete, with coelomic metaplasia, altered immunity, and genetic/epigenetic factors all implicated. 2Staging and the fertility prognosis

Anatomical stage and fertility prognosis are different things — and the anatomical stage predicts fertility poorly.

The long-standing anatomical system is the revised ASRM (rASRM) classification, which scores lesions and adhesions into stages I (minimal) to IV (severe). It is useful for describing disease burden, but it correlates weakly with fertility — a woman with "stage IV" disease may conceive while a "stage I" woman does not.

The Endometriosis Fertility Index (EFI) (Adamson & Pasta, 2010) was developed to fill that gap. Calculated after surgical staging, it combines historical factors (age, duration of infertility, prior pregnancy) with a "least-function score" of the tubes, fimbria, and ovaries, to predict the chance of non-ART conception. It is a genuinely useful prognostic tool and is referenced in the ESHRE guideline for individualising the surgery-versus-ART decision.

3Managing endometriosis-associated infertility

Three evidence points that reliably surprise trainees. Learn them precisely.

Medical suppression does not improve fertility

Counter-intuitively, hormonal suppression of endometriosis (GnRH agonists, the pill, progestins) does not improve natural fertility and delays conception attempts — it treats pain, not subfertility. The ESHRE guideline is explicit: ovarian suppression should not be used to improve fertility. This is a common error worth remembering.

Surgery before IVF is largely obsolete when ART is indicated

A key shift in the 2022 ESHRE guideline: when there is already a clear indication for ART, routine surgery before IVF is no longer recommended — because IVF bypasses the tubal and peritoneal mechanisms anyway, and surgery adds risk without reliably improving ART outcomes. Surgery before IVF is reserved for specific reasons: to treat significant pain, a rapidly growing or suspicious cyst, or when a large endometrioma blocks access to follicles at retrieval. Surgery retains a clearer role for improving spontaneous conception in earlier-stage disease.

The endometrioma–reserve trade-off

When surgery is chosen for an ovarian endometrioma, cystectomy (excising the cyst wall) gives lower recurrence and better spontaneous-pregnancy rates than drainage/coagulation — but it removes normal ovarian tissue and measurably reduces ovarian reserve (a fall in AMH; Somigliana et al., 2012). This creates the central dilemma: operating can help symptoms and spontaneous conception but cost eggs. In women with diminished reserve or bilateral disease, reserve-sparing ablative techniques are considered instead. This is exactly the kind of competing-priorities decision the module is about.

4Male factor beyond the basic semen analysis

Module 2 screened for male factor; this is the specialist workup when it is significant.

Recall the WHO 2021 parameters and the descriptive terms from Module 2 — oligozoospermia, asthenozoospermia, teratozoospermia, and the combined OAT pattern. When male factor is significant, the workup deepens: repeat confirmed analysis, a reproductive and physical examination (testicular volume, presence of the vas, varicocele), and reproductive hormones — chiefly FSH, LH and testosterone, which separate testicular failure from other causes. Sperm DNA fragmentation testing has a role in specific situations (e.g. recurrent loss) but is not a routine first-line test.

The most consequential branch point is azoospermia — no sperm in the ejaculate — because it forces the obstructive-versus-non-obstructive distinction that determines everything downstream.

5Azoospermia: obstructive vs non-obstructive

The central male-factor algorithm, with its mandatory genetic workup.

Confirmed azoospermia (on a repeat, centrifuged sample) is categorised by history, examination, and FSH — not routinely by biopsy. Obstructive azoospermia (OA) reflects normal sperm production with a blockage: typically normal testicular volume and normal FSH, sometimes an absent vas or a low-volume acidic ejaculate. Non-obstructive azoospermia (NOA) reflects a production failure: usually small testes and elevated FSH.

Both mandate a genetic workup before proceeding, because the results change both the retrieval prospects and the counselling:

  • OA / absent vas (CBAVD) → test for CFTR mutations (including the 5T allele). Congenital bilateral absence of the vas is frequently a cystic-fibrosis-spectrum condition, with implications for the couple's offspring, so the partner may need carrier testing too.
  • NOA → karyotype (to detect Klinefelter syndrome, 47,XXY, the commonest genetic cause) and Y-chromosome microdeletion analysis of the AZF regions. The AUA/ASRM guideline recommends Y-microdeletion testing for azoospermia or severe oligospermia (≤1 million/mL) with supporting features.

6Surgical sperm retrieval and ICSI

How azoospermic men can still father children — and the counselling that must accompany it.

Where sperm cannot reach the ejaculate, they are retrieved directly. In OA, sperm are abundant and retrieval is straightforward — PESA (percutaneous epididymal sperm aspiration) or TESA (testicular sperm aspiration). In NOA, sperm are sparse and patchy, so micro-TESE (microdissection testicular sperm extraction) — searching the testis under the operating microscope for the rare productive tubules — gives the best yield. Retrieved sperm are then used with ICSI (Module 1/3): a single sperm injected into each oocyte, which is the only way such small numbers can achieve fertilisation.

◆ Clinical relevance — the counselling is genetic, not just procedural

Surgical retrieval plus ICSI can give a genetically-related child where none was possible a generation ago — but it can also transmit the cause of infertility. A man with a Y-microdeletion passes it to every son, who will likely face the same infertility. A CFTR carrier risks a child with cystic fibrosis if the partner also carries a mutation. Klinefelter carries its own considerations. So the workup is not box-ticking: it drives genetic counselling that a couple needs before committing to retrieval, and it is a recurring theme of the ethics content in Module 6.

7Recurrent implantation failure — and the add-on trap

The area where distressed patients are most over-tested. Know what the evidence actually supports.

Recurrent implantation failure (RIF) — repeated failure of good-quality embryos to implant — is real but frequently over-diagnosed and over-investigated. The crucial reframe: much of what is labelled "implantation failure" is actually expected embryo attrition due to aneuploidy, especially in older women transferring untested embryos. Recall from Module 1 that aneuploidy rises steeply with age; a 40-year-old transferring three untested embryos and not conceiving may simply have transferred three aneuploid embryos, not have an endometrial problem at all.

This matters because RIF drives a marketplace of expensive add-ons — endometrial receptivity testing, immunological therapies, endometrial "scratch" — most with weak or absent evidence. The best trial of endometrial receptivity testing (ERA) found no improvement in live birth versus standard-timed transfer (Doyle et al., JAMA 2022). The rational approach to apparent RIF is to first ensure the basics are right — embryo quality (consider PGT-A or a euploid transfer), an adequate endometrium, no hydrosalpinx or cavity lesion — rather than reaching for unproven tests.

8Diminished reserve, poor response, and donor gametes

When the eggs are the limiting factor — stratifying prognosis and knowing when to change strategy.

Diminished ovarian reserve and poor ovarian response to stimulation are among the hardest situations, because the lever — more or better eggs — is the one hardest to move. Modern practice stratifies these patients with the POSEIDON criteria (Alviggi et al., 2016), which refine the older Bologna definition by combining age, ovarian reserve markers, and prior response into "low-prognosis" groups — a more useful basis for tailoring stimulation and counselling than a single "poor responder" label. Protocol adjustments (higher doses, adjuvants, alternative protocols) can help at the margins, but none manufactures eggs the ovary does not have.

When a woman's own oocytes cannot achieve a reasonable chance — very low reserve, advanced age, repeated failure with aneuploidy, or premature ovarian insufficiency — donor eggs become the option that most changes the odds. Recall the biology from Module 1: because oocyte age drives aneuploidy, young donor eggs largely restore success rates regardless of the recipient's age. It is often the single most effective intervention available — and simultaneously one of the most emotionally and ethically weighty conversations in the clinic.

9The multidisciplinary team and the patient in front of you

Complex cases are team decisions carrying heavy emotional freight. This is EPA 6 and EPA 8.

Complex cases are rarely one clinician's call. The multidisciplinary team — fertility specialists, embryologists, nurses, counsellors, and often urology, genetics, or surgical colleagues — pools expertise on exactly the cases this module covers. Attending the MDT this week, and presenting a case, is your EPA 6 opportunity: it is where you see clinical reasoning done collectively, and where you learn to present a case concisely and identify your own knowledge gaps in real time.

These cases also carry the heaviest psychosocial burden in the clinic. Advanced disease, azoospermia, repeated failure, and the recommendation of donor gametes all land as loss — of a hoped-for outcome, of a genetic connection, of control. Recognising distress, anxiety, grief, and relationship strain, and responding with appropriate support and referral, is EPA 8. Watch how the best clinicians hold the clinical and the human together: they neither retreat into the technical nor abandon accuracy to comfort. In these consultations, the counselling is the medicine.

◆ Worked interpretation — reason before reading the answer
A 40-year-old woman has had three frozen transfers of good-morphology, untested day-5 blastocysts, none resulting in pregnancy. She is distressed and has read online about the "ERA test" and "immune treatment for implantation failure," and asks the clinic to arrange both. Her uterine cavity is normal on recent hysteroscopy; there is no hydrosalpinx. She has two blastocysts remaining.

How would you frame the problem, and what would you advise — and not advise?

Reframe first. At 40, a large proportion of morphologically good embryos are aneuploid (Module 1). Three failed transfers of untested embryos is, statistically, consistent with having transferred aneuploid embryos — this is expected attrition, not necessarily an implantation problem. The normal cavity and absent hydrosalpinx already exclude the common structural endometrial causes.

What the evidence supports. The rational next step is to address embryo competence: consider PGT-A on the remaining/future embryos to select a euploid one, and have an honest prognosis conversation that may include donor eggs given her age. Endometrial receptivity testing (ERA) showed no live-birth benefit in the best RCT (Doyle 2022, JAMA), and immune therapies lack supporting evidence — so recommending them would be spending her money and hope on unproven add-ons.

How to say it (EPA 3 + EPA 8). The skill is declining the add-ons without dismissing her distress: acknowledge how demoralising three failures are, explain in plain terms why the problem is most likely the embryos rather than her uterus, and offer the evidence-based path forward. Validate the feeling, redirect the plan. That integration — honest, kind, and evidence-based — is the whole point of this module.

✓ Module 5 — the concepts you must carry forward
  • Endometriosis impairs fertility by four mechanisms (anatomy, inflammation, ovarian reserve, implantation) — IVF bypasses the first two.
  • Anatomical stage (rASRM) predicts fertility poorly; the EFI is the fertility-prognosis tool.
  • Ovarian suppression does not improve fertility; surgery before IVF is largely obsolete when ART is indicated; cystectomy costs ovarian reserve.
  • Azoospermia splits into OA (normal FSH/testes) and NOA (high FSH/small testes) — both need genetic workup (CFTR for OA/CBAVD; karyotype + Y-microdeletion for NOA).
  • Surgical retrieval + ICSI enables paternity but can transmit the cause — genetic counselling is mandatory. AZFa/AZFb-complete deletions predict failed retrieval.
  • Most "recurrent implantation failure" is embryo aneuploidy, not endometrium — ERA and immune add-ons lack live-birth evidence (JAMA 2022).
  • Donor eggs largely restore success regardless of recipient age (oocyte age drives aneuploidy) — the most effective, and most weighty, option.

◆ How to use this list

The through-line is evidence-based restraint. The ESHRE endometriosis guideline (surgery-before-IVF), the Doyle JAMA trial (ERA), and — from Module 3 — the PGT-A trials all make the same point: intuitively appealing interventions often do not improve live birth, and complex, distressed patients are the ones most exposed to them. Reading these primary sources, rather than a summary, is how you build the judgement to counsel honestly. Module 6 takes this into the ethics and communication of exactly these conversations.
