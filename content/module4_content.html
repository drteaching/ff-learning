Learning to see

Ultrasound is both the primary diagnostic tool and the procedural infrastructure of a fertility clinic. Almost nothing happens without it — reserve is estimated, cycles are monitored, eggs are collected, embryos are transferred, and early pregnancies are confirmed, all under ultrasound. This week you build the spatial vocabulary to look at a scan and say something useful before the report arrives.

The realistic target for a medical student in six weeks is interpretation competence plus supervised basic probe handling — not independent scanning, which takes far longer. By Friday you should be able to identify the normal pelvic structures on a transvaginal scan, count follicles, recognise a trilaminar endometrium, and flag the common pathologies. That is a genuine, transferable skill.

Two habits this week. First, on every scan you attend, form your own interpretation before the sonographer or clinician gives theirs — the same deliberate-practice discipline from Modules 1–3. Second, hold the probe whenever offered under supervision; the motor skill only comes from doing it, and this is EPA 4.

1Principles: getting a usable image

Enough physics to understand resolution, plus the practical choice of probe and orientation.

Ultrasound generates images from reflected high-frequency sound. The key trade-off to understand is frequency versus penetration: higher-frequency probes give better resolution but less depth, lower-frequency probes reach deeper structures but with coarser detail. This directly explains the two probes you will use.

Transvaginal versus transabdominal

Transvaginal ultrasound (TVS) uses a high-frequency probe placed close to the pelvic organs, giving excellent resolution of the uterus and ovaries — it is the workhorse of reproductive medicine and the default for follicle tracking, endometrial assessment, and early pregnancy. Transabdominal ultrasound (TAS) uses a lower-frequency probe through the abdominal wall (with a full bladder as an acoustic window), giving a wider field but lower resolution — used for a large uterus, a mass extending out of the pelvis, or when TVS is not appropriate. For nearly everything in this clinic, TVS is the answer.

Orientation matters for interpretation: on TVS the probe is close to the structures, and you learn to relate the image to the true anatomy — uterine position (anteverted vs retroverted), the relationship of ovaries to the uterus and iliac vessels, and the plane you are scanning in. Disorientation is the commonest early difficulty and resolves with reps.

2The normal pelvis on TVS

Know normal cold, and pathology announces itself. Uterus, endometrium, ovaries, and the antral follicle count.

Uterus and ovaries

The uterus is assessed for size, orientation, myometrial texture (looking for fibroids or adenomyosis), and the endometrium within. The ovaries sit lateral to the uterus, typically near the internal iliac vessels; each is assessed for size, follicle content, and any cyst or mass. Identifying both ovaries reliably is a core early skill and sometimes the hardest — a high or adherent ovary can hide.

The antral follicle count

The antral follicle count (AFC) — from Module 2 — is the sum of small antral follicles (typically 2–10 mm) across both ovaries early in the cycle. With AMH it estimates ovarian reserve and predicts stimulation response. Counting reliably requires a systematic sweep through each ovary; it is operator-dependent, which is one reason AMH (a blood test, operator-independent) has become the co-primary reserve marker.

Follicle tracking during stimulation

This is where Module 3 and Module 4 meet. During a stimulated cycle, serial TVS tracks the growing cohort — counting follicles and measuring the leading ones — to time the trigger (when lead follicles reach ~17–18 mm) and to flag OHSS risk (an excessive count). You will watch this repeatedly this week; try to predict the trigger decision from the scan before it is made.

3Endometrial assessment

The receptive lining, seen on ultrasound. Thickness and pattern, tied to Module 1's implantation window.

The endometrium is assessed for thickness (measured as the double-layer thickness in the sagittal plane) and pattern. In the proliferative phase, oestrogen produces a trilaminar or "triple-line" appearance — three parallel lines reflecting the layered structure — which is the pattern associated with receptivity. After ovulation, progesterone transforms it to a homogeneously echogenic (bright) secretory endometrium.

For implantation, a periovulatory endometrium that is trilaminar and at least ~7 mm is generally associated with better outcomes; a persistently thin lining (<7 mm) is associated with lower implantation rates. Treat these as associations, not hard cut-offs — the evidence is observational and thin endometria still achieve pregnancies. In the FET timing you learned in Module 3, the endometrium is built with oestrogen (aiming for an adequate trilaminar lining) before progesterone is started and the transfer counted.

4Fibroids and the FIGO classification

Not all fibroids matter for fertility — location is everything. The FIGO system tells you which to worry about.

Uterine fibroids (leiomyomas) are common and mostly incidental, but their effect on fertility depends almost entirely on their relationship to the endometrial cavity. The FIGO leiomyoma classification (Munro et al., part of the PALM-COEIN system) numbers fibroids 0–8 by location. The critical distinction for reproductive medicine: submucosal fibroids (types 0–2) that distort or contact the cavity are the ones that impair implantation and warrant treatment (usually hysteroscopic resection). Intramural fibroids not distorting the cavity (types 3–5) have a smaller and debated effect; subserosal fibroids (types 6–7) generally do not affect fertility.

5Endometrioma and polycystic ovarian morphology

Two ovarian appearances every reproductive sonographer must recognise.

Endometrioma

An endometrioma ("chocolate cyst") is an ovarian cyst of ectopic endometrial tissue — the ovarian manifestation of endometriosis (Module 5). Its classic ultrasound appearance is a cyst with homogeneous, low-level "ground-glass" internal echoes and no solid vascular components. Recognising it matters before IVF: endometriomas can reduce the ovarian reserve of the affected ovary, and cystectomy — while sometimes indicated — itself removes normal ovarian tissue and can lower reserve. The decision to operate before IVF is nuanced and case-specific, and you will see it discussed in Module 5.

Polycystic ovarian morphology (PCOM)

Recall PCOS from Modules 1–2. On ultrasound, PCOM is the appearance of numerous small antral follicles, classically arranged peripherally ("string of pearls") around increased central stroma. The threshold matters and has been updated: the 2023 international guideline defines PCOM as a follicle number per ovary of ≥20 (follicles 2–9 mm) in at least one ovary when using a high-frequency transducer (bandwidth including 8 MHz). With older/lower-frequency equipment or poor image quality, an ovarian volume ≥10 mL is used instead. Importantly, the 2023 guideline also permits an elevated AMH as an alternative marker of PCOM — reflecting how closely AMH tracks the antral follicle pool (Module 1).

6Hydrosalpinx

A finding that changes the IVF plan before the cycle starts — recall Module 2.

A hydrosalpinx — a blocked, fluid-filled fallopian tube — appears on ultrasound as a tubular, fluid-filled adnexal structure, sometimes with a folded or "cog-wheel" appearance, separate from the ovary. As covered in Module 2, its importance is that the fluid is toxic to embryos and roughly halves IVF success if it communicates with the uterine cavity. Recognising a hydrosalpinx on the pre-IVF scan is what triggers the surgical decision (salpingectomy or proximal occlusion) before transfer — a clear example of how a scan finding reshapes the treatment plan rather than merely describing anatomy.

7Early pregnancy ultrasound

Confirming location, dating, and viability — with strict criteria that exist to avoid harming a viable pregnancy.

After a positive pregnancy test, TVS answers three questions: is the pregnancy intrauterine (excluding ectopic), how many, and is it viable. The normal early landmarks appear in a predictable sequence:

  • Gestational sac — visible from ~4.5–5 weeks.
  • Yolk sac — appears within the sac at ~5.5 weeks; the first definite sign of an intrauterine pregnancy.
  • Embryo (fetal pole) with cardiac activity — by ~6 weeks; cardiac motion is usually seen once the embryo is ≥2 mm.

Diagnosing non-viability — the strict criteria

Because a false diagnosis of miscarriage could end a wanted, viable pregnancy, the criteria for calling non-viability are deliberately conservative and specific (Doubilet et al., 2013; adopted by the Society of Radiologists in Ultrasound and equivalents in the UK and Australia). On a transvaginal scan, a pregnancy is definitively non-viable when:

  • an embryo with crown-rump length ≥7 mm has no cardiac activity, or
  • a gestational sac with mean sac diameter ≥25 mm contains no embryo, or
  • there is no embryo with a heartbeat ≥2 weeks after a scan showing a sac without a yolk sac (or ≥11 days after a scan showing a sac with a yolk sac).

Findings below these thresholds (e.g. CRL <7 mm with no heartbeat, or an empty sac of MSD 16–24 mm) are suspicious but not diagnostic — they mandate a follow-up scan, not a diagnosis. Getting this discipline right is exactly the "don't over-call" judgement the criteria were built to enforce.

◆ Clinical relevance — ectopic pregnancy

An ectopic pregnancy must always be considered when the pregnancy test is positive but no intrauterine gestational sac is seen — particularly in a woman with pain, bleeding, or risk factors (prior ectopic, tubal disease, IVF itself). Ultrasound signs include an adnexal mass separate from the ovary, a tubal ring, or free fluid; correlation with serial β-hCG is essential. Ectopic pregnancy is a potential emergency, and "pregnancy of unknown location" — positive test, nothing seen — is a recognised, actively managed clinical state, not a reassurance to send the patient home.

8Procedural ultrasound

Ultrasound is not only diagnostic — it guides the clinic's two core procedures.

Two procedures you met in Module 3 are ultrasound-guided. Oocyte retrieval (OPU) is performed under continuous transvaginal ultrasound, the needle guided into each follicle in real time. Embryo transfer is frequently done under transabdominal ultrasound guidance, visualising the catheter to place the embryo optimally within the cavity. Seeing ultrasound used this way — as the eyes of a procedure, not just a diagnostic snapshot — completes the picture of why it is the indispensable tool of the unit.

◆ Worked interpretation — decide before reading the answer
A woman conceives after a frozen embryo transfer. At what she believes is ~6 weeks, a transvaginal scan shows a single intrauterine gestational sac with a mean sac diameter of 22 mm, containing a yolk sac but no visible embryo. She is asymptomatic. The sonographer asks what you would report.

Work through it against the criteria before deciding. Is this diagnostic of non-viability, suspicious, or normal? What is the correct next step?

Interpretation. Apply the thresholds. The mean sac diameter is 22 mm — below the ≥25 mm cut-off for calling an empty sac non-viable. There is a yolk sac but no embryo yet. This does not meet any definitive non-viability criterion. It is, at most, suspicious but not diagnostic.

Critical point. The temptation — "6 weeks, no embryo, must be failing" — is exactly the over-call the criteria exist to prevent. The stated dates in an IVF pregnancy should be precise (transfer date is known), but even so, a 22 mm sac with a yolk sac and no embryo has not reached the threshold at which non-viability can be declared.

Correct action. Report as an intrauterine pregnancy of uncertain viability and arrange a follow-up scan after the guideline interval (≥11 days after a scan showing a sac with a yolk sac). Do not diagnose miscarriage, and counsel the patient accordingly — hopeful but honest about the uncertainty. This is EPA 7: communicating an uncertain finding without either false reassurance or premature bad news.

✓ Module 4 — the concepts you must carry forward
  • TVS is the workhorse: high frequency, high resolution, close to the pelvic organs. Higher frequency = better resolution, less depth.
  • Know the normal pelvis cold — uterus, trilaminar endometrium (≥7 mm periovulatory is favourable), ovaries, and the AFC.
  • Follicle tracking times the trigger (~17–18 mm) and flags OHSS risk — this scan drives Module 3's decisions.
  • Fibroids: location decides relevance. FIGO submucosal types 0–2 impair fertility and are resected; subserosal usually do not.
  • PCOM = ≥20 follicles per ovary (≥8 MHz probe) or volume ≥10 mL, or elevated AMH — one Rotterdam criterion, non-specific alone.
  • Early-pregnancy non-viability is strictly defined: CRL ≥7 mm no cardiac, or MSD ≥25 mm no embryo. Below that → follow-up, never a diagnosis.
  • A hydrosalpinx or endometrioma seen on ultrasound changes the plan before IVF, not after.
