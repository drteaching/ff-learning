Module 4 — Open-Licensed Image Sourcing Guide

Real ultrasound images to sit alongside the schematic figures — with licences you can actually use

The schematic SVGs in Module 4 teach orientation and concept; real ultrasound images teach recognition. This guide points to genuinely open-licensed images for each topic, with the licence to verify and an attribution template. It is a sourcing map, not a set of embedded files — for the reasons in the two boxes below.

◆ The licence that matters for your course

This rotation is intended to seed an accredited — and potentially paid — programme. That makes commercial use the relevant test. Two Creative Commons clauses break commercial use: NC (non-commercial) and ND (no derivatives — which also blocks annotating or cropping). So Radiopaedia (CC BY-NC-SA) and StatPearls / NCBI Bookshelf figures (typically CC BY-NC-ND) are excellent for teaching but not safe once the course is commercial or you annotate them. Use them only if the course stays genuinely free, and even then ND blocks editing.

✓ What is safe (with attribution)

For a commercial course, prefer Public Domain / CC0 (no restrictions), CC BY (attribution only), and CC BY-SA (attribution + you must license your derivative work under the same terms — a real obligation, so read it before building on the image). All three permit commercial use and annotation. Wikimedia Commons is the best single source because each file's licence is stated plainly on its page.

◆ Why this is a guide, not embedded images

Commons licenses each file individually, and I could not machine-verify every file's licence box in this session. Embedding images whose licence I hadn't confirmed would be exactly the IP exposure to avoid. So: download each candidate from the file page, read its licence box, confirm it's PD/CC BY/CC BY-SA, then annotate and embed with the credit line. Budget ten minutes per image.

The reliable shortcut: Mikael Häggström

A large share of the usable pelvic and obstetric ultrasound images on Commons were contributed by Mikael Häggström, a physician who releases most of his medical images into the public domain (CC0). When a candidate file is his, it is the lowest-friction option — no attribution legally required (though crediting is courteous), commercial use fine, annotation fine. His Commons user gallery is a good first stop, and his images populate the OHSS, early-pregnancy, and endometrium examples above.

Secondary source: PMC open-access figures

Open-access articles in PubMed Central often carry CC BY figures (commercial use fine with attribution) — useful for the harder-to-find appearances (endometrioma, hydrosalpinx, specific fibroid types). The trap: the OA subset also contains CC BY-NC and CC BY-NC-ND articles. Check each article's licence statement (usually at the end), and note that individual figures can carry a different licence from the article ("third-party material" credit lines). Only lift a figure when the article — and that specific figure — is CC BY.

Attribution template

For any CC BY or CC BY-SA image, include a credit line near the image or in a figure-credits list:

[Author/username], "[File title]", via Wikimedia Commons, licensed under [CC BY-SA 4.0] ([licence URL]). [Cropped / annotated for teaching.]

For CC BY-SA, remember the share-alike obligation: if the image is integrated such that your figure is a derivative, that derivative may need to carry a compatible licence — usually fine for standalone teaching figures, but worth a glance if the image is heavily built upon. For Public Domain / CC0, no credit line is legally required; a courtesy credit is still good practice.

Recommended workflow

  • Shortlist from the Commons categories above; prefer Häggström (PD) and CC BY / CC BY-SA files.
  • Open the file page and read the licence box — do not trust the category listing alone.
  • Download the full-resolution file; annotate (label the yolk sac, the submucosal fibroid, the string of pearls) to match the teaching point.
  • Embed with the credit line and keep a figure-credits register for the module (useful for accreditation review).
  • Avoid Radiopaedia and StatPearls figures for the commercial version — keep a note of why, so a future reviewer doesn't reintroduce them.
◆ One quality caveat

Commons image quality is variable — some files are low-resolution or poorly labelled. For a flagship teaching resource, the best long-term move is to build your own de-identified image library from Flinders Fertility scans (with the consent/de-identification protocol already flagged for the rotation). Your own images have no licence question, show your own equipment and reporting conventions, and can be captioned to your teaching points exactly. Open-licensed images are the bridge until that library exists.
